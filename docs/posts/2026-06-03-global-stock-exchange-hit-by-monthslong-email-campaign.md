## ⚡ Quick Summary
A recent cyberattack has come to light where a threat actor managed to gain continuous access to the email inbox of a high-profile finance executive at a global stock exchange. This was achieved through the clever use of legitimate, native Windows tools, highlighting the evolving sophistication of cyber threats. By leveraging tools that are inherently trusted by security software, the attacker was able to remain under the radar for an extended period, gaining valuable insights into sensitive financial information.

## 🛠 Technical Analysis
From a technical standpoint, the use of native Windows tools by the threat actor signifies a shift towards living off the land (LOTL) tactics. This approach allows attackers to blend in with normal system activity, making detection by traditional security measures more challenging. The fact that the attacker was able to maintain access for months underscores the importance of continuous monitoring and advanced threat detection capabilities. Moreover, it emphasizes the need for organizations to implement robust email security measures and to educate executives about the risks associated with email-based attacks. The exploitation of trust in native tools also points to the potential vulnerabilities in assuming that all activities conducted by built-in system tools are benign.

## 🛡 Mitigation & Impact
To mitigate such attacks, organizations should consider implementing multi-factor authentication (MFA) for email access, enhancing email content scanning for malicious code or phishing attempts, and regularly auditing email accounts for suspicious activity. Additionally, conducting regular security awareness training for executives and employees can help prevent initial compromise. The impact of this breach is significant, not only due to the potential financial losses but also the reputational damage to the stock exchange. It serves as a wake-up call for the financial sector to reassess its cybersecurity posture, particularly focusing on email security and the detection of stealthy, low-and-slow attacks that leverage trusted system tools. Implementing a zero-trust model, where all users and devices are verified, and enforcing the principle of least privilege can further reduce the attack surface and the potential for prolonged, undetected access.
